Relieve

A Brand Style Guide

I. The Relieve Philosophy: Precision, Nature, and Clarity

1.1 Our Mission: Mapping the Intersection

The mission of Relieve is to create objects and communications that celebrate the intersection of natural topography and human-made precision. The brand does not merely represent nature; it interprets it through a lens of clarity, function, and minimalist beauty. This approach is rooted in the belief that design should serve as a tool for clear communication rather than purely as a vehicle for personal expression. In this context, Relieve undertakes a cultural mission: to influence taste by making exceptional, functional design genuinely accessible, focusing on the public and personal spaces where people and history meet. Our work---from map posters to digital interfaces---is a testament to the idea that the complex beauty of the natural world can be understood and appreciated through the deliberate application of order and structure.

1.2 The Name: Relief in Two Forms

The brand name, Relieve, operates on a deliberate duality of meaning, forming the conceptual core of our identity. This duality is the source of the brand's creative tension and, ultimately, its harmony. Every design decision must serve both facets of this identity, balancing objective data with subjective experience.

  • Topographic Relief: This refers to the physical shape, texture, and quantitative representation of the land's surface. It is the world of data, cartography, elevation, and geology---objective, measurable, and scientific. It speaks to our commitment to accuracy and precision in everything we create, from the contours on a map to the structure of a web page.
  • Emotional Relief: This refers to the feeling of calm, clarity, and escape found in both the vastness of nature and the quiet order of well-executed design. It is the human experience of hiking a trail, the serenity of a minimalist space, and the satisfaction of understanding complex information presented with elegance. This aspect of the name guides our aesthetic toward tranquility and respect for the user's cognitive load.

1.3 Our Voice: Objective, Calm, and Assured

The Relieve brand voice is a reflection of its dual identity. Communication should be executed with the precision of a cartographer and the quiet confidence of a seasoned hiker. The language used is clear, concise, and devoid of hyperbole or unnecessary ornamentation. This commitment to objectivity is a direct extension of the Swiss Style philosophy, which prioritizes the clear sharing of information. The tone is always assured but never arrogant, calm but never passive. It embodies the Bauhaus principle of "less is more," where effectiveness is achieved through simplicity and directness.

II. Core Principles: A Foundation in Functionalism

The visual language of Relieve is built upon a synthesis of influential 20th-century design movements, updated with a contemporary European sensibility. These principles are not arbitrary rules but a philosophical framework that ensures every creation is purposeful, clear, and timeless.

2.1 The Swiss International Style: The Grid as Our Guide

The Swiss Style, or International Typographic Style, provides the structural backbone for the Relieve brand. Its emphasis on objectivity, legibility, and order aligns perfectly with our mission to present complex information with absolute clarity. We adopt its core tenets as our own:

  • The Grid System: The grid is the fundamental, invisible structure for all layouts. It is a mathematically derived framework that ensures proportional harmony and a clear visual hierarchy among all elements, from text to imagery. It is the primary tool for bringing order to information.
  • Sans-Serif Typography: We exclusively use sans-serif typefaces for their inherent clarity, modernity, and objectivity. By removing decorative elements, the focus remains on the content itself, ensuring maximum readability.
  • Asymmetrical Layouts: Compositions are arranged asymmetrically to create dynamic visual interest and to guide the viewer's eye in a natural, logical flow. This approach, often paired with left-aligned, ragged-right text, respects the natural reading direction of Western languages.
  • Objective Imagery: Photography is treated as a functional element, a clear window onto the subject. Images are documentary in style, presenting landscapes and objects with neutrality and precision, allowing the subject to speak for itself.

2.2 The Bauhaus School: The Purity of Form

The Bauhaus movement informs the brand's philosophical approach to creation, emphasizing the unity of aesthetics and utility. It provides the "why" behind our minimalist forms and material choices.

  • Form Follows Function: This is our guiding creed. The aesthetic of any object or design must be dictated by its purpose. Every line, shape, and color serves a function; unnecessary ornamentation is eliminated to enhance clarity and usability.
  • Geometric Essentialism: Our visual language is built from fundamental geometric shapes---circles, squares, triangles, and lines. This approach, central to Bauhaus teaching, ensures a cohesive and universally understandable system, from our iconography to our layout structures.
  • Unity of Art & Technology: We embrace the Bauhaus ideal of bridging the gap between art, craft, and industry. The precision of a 3D printer and the artistry of a cartographer are seen as parts of a single, unified creative process, aimed at producing functional, accessible, and beautiful objects.

2.3 Contemporary European Minimalism: The Human Touch

To prevent our functionalist approach from becoming sterile, we temper it with the sensibilities of contemporary European design. This involves a focus on materiality, light, and creating a sense of calm. Our minimalism is not cold; it is designed from a human perspective, aiming to create "poetic objects" and serene spaces. We draw inspiration from Spanish and Scandinavian design, which masterfully use natural light, earthy materials, and uncluttered layouts to create environments that are both minimalist and deeply inviting.

2.4 The Active Role of Whitespace

In the Relieve design system, whitespace (or negative space) is not an empty background; it is a critical, active design element. Its function is manifold: it enhances readability, creates focus on key elements, and establishes a visual hierarchy.

More significantly, we treat whitespace as a direct cartographic and thematic element. It is the visual equivalent of the vast, open landscapes that inspire the brand. Just as a cartographer uses the unmapped ocean or the empty sky to give scale and context to landmasses, we use whitespace to give our content room to breathe. It evokes the feeling of open space, of majesty, and of the emotional relief that comes from an uncluttered vista. This philosophy dictates that our layouts---from website pages to poster designs---should feel expansive and balanced, using generous margins and spacing to guide the eye and calm the mind.

IV. Typography: The Voice of Relieve

Typography is the primary vehicle for our brand voice. The system is built on a foundation of objectivity and clarity, adhering to the principles of the International Typographic Style. Our approach is not merely aesthetic; it is a functional system where the choice of typeface itself communicates the nature of the information being presented.

4.1 Our Typefaces: A Dual System

The Relieve brand employs a dual-typeface system to tell its complete story. This pairing is a narrative device, reflecting the brand's core tension between the human experience of nature and the technical science of geography. Each typeface has a distinct role, and their interplay creates a rich, functional, and hierarchical typographic language.

  • The Humanist Voice: A clear, modern geometric sans-serif is used for headlines, body copy, and primary brand communication. It is approachable, legible, and embodies our principles of minimalist design.
  • The Technical Voice: A characterful monospace font is used for technical data, captions, coordinates, and annotations. Its fixed-width nature evokes precision, data, and the craft of cartography.

4.2 Primary Typeface: Geist Sans

Geist Sans is the primary typeface for the Relieve brand. It is a modern geometric sans-serif designed with "precision, clarity, and functionality at its core" and draws direct inspiration from the Swiss design movement. Its clean forms, excellent legibility at all sizes, and neutral-yet-confident tone make it the perfect vehicle for our main brand voice. It is used for all headlines, sub-headlines, and primary body text.

4.3 Secondary & Technical Typeface: Space Mono

Space Mono is the secondary and technical typeface. As a monospace font, each character occupies an equal amount of horizontal space, lending it a rhythmic, mechanical quality ideal for displaying data. Space Mono was chosen for its geometric foundation, which pairs harmoniously with Geist Sans, and for its unique character details that prevent it from feeling sterile. It is used for captions, map labels, data tables, code snippets, and any instance where a technical or editorial feel is desired.

4.5 Application Rules

To ensure optimal readability and adherence to our design principles, the following typesetting rules must be applied:

  • Alignment: All text should be left-aligned with a ragged-right margin. This is a core tenet of the International Typographic Style, enhancing readability by providing a consistent starting point for each line and avoiding the uneven spacing caused by justification. Centered text should be used only in rare, specific cases for emphasis (e.g., a single-line poster title).
  • Line Length: For body copy, line length should be maintained between 50 and 75 characters (including spaces) for optimal reading rhythm. On the web, this translates to a maximum width of approximately 720 pixels.
  • Paragraph Spacing: Use space between paragraphs instead of first-line indents. A single line break (equal to the body text's font size) is sufficient to separate paragraphs clearly.
  • Hyphenation: Automated hyphenation should be enabled to ensure the ragged-right edge remains balanced and does not create distracting shapes in the negative space.

V. Colour Palette: Hues of the Earth & Data

The Relieve color system is an exercise in systematic naturalism. The palettes are derived from the hues of the natural world---rock, water, forest, and sky---but they are organized and applied with the rigor of a scientific system. This approach allows color to serve a dual purpose: to evoke the emotion of a landscape and to encode complex information with clarity and precision.

The entire color system is structured like a cartographic legend. The Primary Palette forms the "base map"---the fundamental tones of the earth. The Secondary & Accent Palette provides the "points of interest"---colors used for highlighting and guidance. Finally, the specialized Cartographic & Data Palettes act as "thematic overlays," designed for the functional purpose of visualizing quantitative and qualitative information.

5.4 Colour Ratios & Application

To maintain the minimalist and balanced aesthetic, color should be applied according to a simple ratio. This prevents the overuse of accent colors and ensures that compositions remain clean and focused. The recommended ratio is an adaptation of the classic 60-30-10 rule:

  • 60% Primary Neutral (Paper): The dominant color, used for backgrounds and creating ample whitespace.
  • 30% Secondary Neutral (Dark Slate / Warm Stone): Used for text, structural elements, and secondary backgrounds.
  • 10% Accent Colour (Alpine Blue, etc.): Used for calls-to-action, highlights, and key data points.

VI. The Grid: Structuring Information

The grid system is the single most important tool for achieving the clarity, order, and harmony that defines the Relieve brand. It is the invisible scaffolding upon which all our designs are built, a direct application of the Swiss Style's most fundamental principle. The grid is not a constraint on creativity; it is a framework that enables it, ensuring that every element is placed with intention and purpose.

6.1 The Master Grid

The Relieve master grid is a 12-column grid. This structure was chosen for its supreme flexibility. A 12-column grid can be easily divided into halves (6 columns), thirds (4 columns), quarters (3 columns), or sixths (2 columns), allowing for a wide variety of balanced and asymmetrical layouts while maintaining a consistent underlying structure. The space between columns, known as the gutter, is essential for separating content and creating visual rhythm.

VII. Imagery & Iconography: A Visual Language

The visual language of Relieve extends beyond typography and color to include photography and iconography. These elements must adhere to the same principles of objectivity, clarity, and minimalist beauty.

7.1 Photography Style: The Objective Observer

Photography for the Relieve brand is functional, serving to document and display our subjects with honesty and clarity. It avoids overt stylization in favor of a neutral, documentary perspective, as championed by the Swiss Style. The beauty is found in the subject itself, not in heavy-handed post-processing.

Subject: Expansive landscapes, topographic textures (rock, snow, flora), the process of making (3D printers, cartography tools), and solitary human figures viewed from a distance to emphasize scale.

Composition: Asymmetrical, adhering to the rule of thirds. A strong emphasis on negative space (sky, fog, water) is crucial to evoke a sense of openness and align with our principle of active whitespace.

Tone: Documentary, objective, and neutral. Avoid overly dramatic, high-contrast, or saturated styles. The lighting should feel natural and authentic to the environment.

Colour: All photography must harmonize with the brand's established color palette. Images should feature muted, natural tones that complement our Paper, Slate, and Stone neutrals, with occasional accents of Alpine Blue or Conifer Green.

7.2 Iconography System: Geometric Purity

Our iconography is a direct expression of Bauhaus principles: functional, minimalist, and constructed from basic geometric shapes. Each icon is built on a 16x16 pixel grid to ensure consistency and scalability. The style is simple, clean, and instantly recognizable, serving as a universal language for navigation and information.

IX. Application: Cartographic & Data Design

The design of our core products---map posters and data visualizations---is where the Relieve philosophy is most tangibly expressed. Here, we synthesize functional, data-driven cartography with a minimalist artistic aesthetic. The goal is to strip away all non-essential "chartjunk" to allow the data itself, and the natural patterns within it, to become the primary visual element.

9.1 Map Poster Design Rules

A Relieve map poster is both a precise piece of geographic information and a minimalist work of art. The design prioritizes the topography itself, using a restrained visual system to present the data with elegance and clarity. This approach treats the map as a piece of data art, where the "data-ink ratio" is maximized by focusing on the essential contour lines.

Mount Rainier

46.8523° N, 121.7603° W

  • Contour Lines: Use the Warm Stone or Dark Slate color for high contrast against the Paper background. Line weights must be differentiated: a thicker weight (e.g., 1.5pt) for index contours and a thinner weight (e.g., 0.5pt) for intermediate contours, consistent with topographic standards.
  • Hydrography (Water): All water features (lakes, rivers, oceans) are rendered in Alpine Blue.
  • Typography: All labels (place names, elevation markers, coordinates) must use Space Mono for its technical clarity. A strict typographic hierarchy must be followed for scale and importance.
  • Symbols: Use the approved geometric icon set for points of interest (e.g., a triangle for a summit, a location pin for a trailhead).
  • Layout: The composition must feature generous margins, treating the whitespace as a framing device. The title, coordinates, and any legend information are placed cleanly at the bottom of the poster, aligned to the underlying grid.

9.2 Charts & Data Visualization Rules

All charts and graphs created for the brand must adhere to the principles of minimalist data visualization. The guiding philosophy, articulated by experts like Edward Tufte, is to maximize the "data-ink ratio"---that is, to dedicate the majority of the visual elements to displaying the data itself, and to remove everything else.

  • Strategic Use of Colour: Use color purposefully, not decoratively. Employ the approved Data Visualization Palettes to encode information. Use a neutral color for the base data and an accent color to highlight a key finding.

X. Application: Print & Physical Media

The Relieve brand extends into the tangible world through printed posters and 3D-printed objects. The same principles of minimalism, functionalism, and material honesty must guide these creations.

10.1 Poster Layouts

Beyond map posters, promotional or event posters are exercises in pure Swiss and Bauhaus typographic composition. These designs rely on the dramatic interplay between bold, asymmetrically placed typography, a single powerful image, and generous, active whitespace. The layout is always structured on a grid, inspired by the iconic works of masters like Josef Müller-Brockmann and Armin Hofmann.

10.2 3D Printing Considerations

Our 3D-printed topographic models are the ultimate physical manifestation of the brand's ethos. They are the most direct expression of "form follows function," where the form of the object is the data. The design is nothing more than the honest, unadorned representation of geographic information in three dimensions.

  • Materiality: Recommended materials should align with our natural and minimalist palette. Matte filaments in shades of white, grey, and black are preferred. Wood or stone-composite filaments can also be used to enhance the tactile, earthy quality of the objects.
  • Form: The focus must remain on the accuracy and detail of the topographic data. The 3D model is not a sculpture of a mountain; it is the mountain, in miniature. No additional decorative elements should be added. This practice directly connects to the Bauhaus principle of uniting art and technology to create functional, mass-producible objects. The 3D printer is our modern workshop.
